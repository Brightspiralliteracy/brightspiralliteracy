What to Notice: A Parent's Reading Checklist

For families wondering whether what they're seeing at home is worth paying attention to.

Read the checklist

You know your child. You know the difference between a hard week and a pattern. This checklist is for parents who keep noticing something, even when the school says everything is fine.

It won't give you a diagnosis. It will give you language for what you're already seeing, so you can trust your instincts and know what questions to ask.

1
How they approach unfamiliar words
They guess at words using the first letter or the picture rather than working through the sounds
They skip words they don't recognize and keep going
They read the same unfamiliar word differently each time it appears on the page
What this tells you

A child who is truly decoding uses the sounds in the word, not context clues. Guessing from pictures or first letters is a coping strategy, not a reading strategy. It works in early kindergarten and stops working around second grade.

2
What happens when they read aloud
Reading is slow and labored, or too fast and flat with no attention to meaning
They can read the words accurately but can't tell you what they just read
They resist reading aloud or find reasons to stop early
What this tells you

Fluency and comprehension are connected. A child working very hard to decode individual words has very little mental energy left for understanding the text. Avoidance almost always means the task feels harder than it looks from the outside.

3
Writing and spelling
Spelling is inconsistent, the same word spelled differently in the same paragraph
Sentences are short and simple even when they can talk about the same topic in detail
They know what they want to say but it doesn't come out on the page
What this tells you

Spelling and reading are two sides of the same skill. A child who cannot hold the sounds of a word in sequence to read it will also struggle to hold them in sequence to spell it. The gap between how a child talks and how they write is often a reliable signal.

4
How they feel about reading
They say "I'm not a reader" or "I hate reading" with real feeling, not just preference
They were keeping up in earlier grades and things feel harder now
They describe themselves as bad at school or avoid talking about it
What this tells you

Reader identity forms early and is hard to rebuild once it's set. A child who has tried and failed to decode accurately will eventually stop trying. When you hear "I'm stupid" or "reading is boring," something about the instruction has not worked for this particular child.

5
The school picture vs. what you see at home
The school says they are making progress, but reading at home is still a struggle
Progress feels invisible, no clear sense of what they can do now that they couldn't three months ago
You've tried an app or a generic tutor and the results were hard to see
What this tells you

Schools measure progress against group norms. A child can be "within the normal range" on a school report and still be working much harder than their peers to produce the same output. What you see at homework time is real data.
